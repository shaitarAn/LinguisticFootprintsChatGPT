In Ausonius’ Cento nuptialis, specifically in the passage concerning the defloration of the bride (Cento 101–131), there is a clever reference to ancient criticism of Vergil and, more specifically, the discourse on the moral integrity of the great poet and his work. By skillfully incorporating implicit and explicit references to those critics who were marginalized as hypocrites and pedants within the scholastic tradition (the obtrectatores Vergilii), Ausonius playfully neutralizes the pornographic-obscene nature of this part of his wedding cento. These references serve a central apologetic function by satirically exaggerating a particular reception attitude and deflecting potential accusations of obscenity back onto the reader.
