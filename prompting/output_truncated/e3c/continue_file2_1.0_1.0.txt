On physical examination, the patient appeared pale and slightly emaciated. There were no signs of jaundice or any other abnormal findings on her skin. Her vital signs were stable, with a blood pressure of 120/80 mmHg and heart rate of 80 beats per minute. The abdominal examination revealed tenderness in the epigastric region, without any obvious masses or organomegaly.

Given her history of weight loss and persistent abdominal pain, a thorough evaluation was initiated to identify the cause. Laboratory tests were ordered, including complete blood count (CBC), liver function tests (LFTs), amylase and lipase levels, and coagulation profile. The CBC revealed mild anemia with a hemoglobin level of 10 g/dL. LFTs were within normal limits, ruling out liver dysfunction as a cause for her symptoms.

An ultrasound scan was also performed to assess the abdomen. It showed no evidence of gallstones or other abnormalities in the biliary tree or liver parenchyma. However, it did reveal an enlarged pancreas with diffuse heterogeneity consistent with pancreatic pathology.

To further investigate the pancreatic abnormalities seen on ultrasound, a computed tomography (CT) scan was ordered. The CT scan confirmed an enlarged pancreas with irregular margins and heterogeneous enhancement suggestive of chronic pancreatitis. Additionally, dilatation of the main pancreatic duct was noted without any evident obstructing lesions.

The patient's diagnosis was established as chronic pancreatitis likely due to alcohol consumption based on her history prior to pregnancy where she admitted excessive alcohol intake in social gatherings despite limited available medical records regarding this habit during pregnancy thus far.

Treatment options for chronic pancreatitis during pregnancy are limited due to potential harm to both mother and fetus from invasive procedures or medications commonly used in non-pregnant individuals such as surgery or opioid analgesics for pain control respectively.

We decided that conservative management would be best suited for this patient at this gestation, focusing on pain management, nutritional support, and close monitoring for any signs of complications. The patient was started on pancreatic enzyme replacement therapy (PERT) to aid in digestion and absorption of nutrients. She was also referred to a nutritionist who designed a high-calorie diet plan with frequent small meals to ensure adequate nutrition for both the mother and the fetus.

Regular antenatal check-ups were scheduled to monitor fetal growth and check for any signs of intrauterine growth restriction (IUGR) or preterm labor. Maternal well-being was also closely monitored, with emphasis on pain control using non-opioid analgesics such as acetaminophen or dipyrone.

Close communication between the obstetrician-gynecologist, gastroenterologist, anesthesiologist, and neonatologist was maintained throughout the course of her pregnancy to provide optimal care while minimizing risks. The patient's emotional well-being was also addressed by involving a social worker or psychologist as needed.

The patient had regular follow-up appointments until delivery at 38 weeks gestation when she presented with spontaneous rupture of membranes. She underwent an uncomplicated vaginal delivery resulting in a healthy baby boy without any immediate postnatal complications.
