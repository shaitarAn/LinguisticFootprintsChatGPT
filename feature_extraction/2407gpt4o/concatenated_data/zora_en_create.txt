**Imagining the Future: Predicting Language Proficiency in Multilingual Settings Based on Students‚Äô Initial Attitudes Towards Different Languages**
**Abstract:** The swift tide of globalization necessitates proficiency in multiple languages, particularly in multilingual societies. Although motivational factors and value beliefs have been explored across various language learning contexts, limited research has focused on how initial attitudes toward different languages might predict long-term language proficiency. This study embarks upon an investigation into whether Swiss German students‚Äô initial attitudes towards learning English, French, and German at the onset of upper secondary school can forecast their ultimate proficiency levels in these languages by graduation. We employ a longitudinal design using hierarchical linear modeling to analyze data from 700 students (M age = 15.60 years; SD = .59; 52% female) collected annually from Grades 9 through 11. Our findings indicate that initial positive attitudes towards English are predictive of not only higher proficiency growth rates in English but also ancillary benefits for French and German over time. Furthermore, we find subtle yet significant distinctions between multilingual and monolingual learners' trajectories of language development rooted in their attitudinal foundations.

# The Role of Sociophonetic Variation in the Perception of Vowel Quality Differences Between Alemannic Dialects and Swiss Standard German
## Abstract
While a considerable body of research has delved into the phonetic properties of Alemannic (ALM) dialects, attention to vowel perception ‚Äî the listeners' ability to distinguish between subtly different vowel qualities ‚Äî remains scarce. This study embarks on an exploratory path focusing on sociophonetic variation and its influence on how speakers of ALM dialects perceive vowels in both ALM and Swiss Standard German (SSG). Specifically, this study aims to evaluate if there is a perceptual preference or perceptibility bias among native ALM speakers when listening to vowels produced by SSG speakers from different regions. The analysis involves 32 participants from Bern, Chur, Brig, and Zurich who were subjected to an auditory discrimination task involving synthesized vowels characteristic for both their own and other dialectal regions. The outcomes underscore significant differences in vowel perception depending upon the listener's regional background, thereby offering an incisive understanding into how sociolinguistic factors shape linguistic phenomena.

Landscapes, embodying vast topographies, ecosystems, and naturally occurring phenomena, underpin the quality of human life by furnishing essential material resources such as food and water alongside indispensable nonmaterial benefits like aesthetic pleasure and emotional tranquility. These landscapes' significance is globally recognized through international conventions and treaties that obligate signatories to safeguard, monitor, and administrate all landscapes equitably (MEA 2005). Despite their universally acknowledged importance, there exists a considerable information lacuna concerning how individuals from multilingual backgrounds conceive 'landscape'‚Äîan understanding imperative for effective landscape management.

Abstract:
In this paper, we investigate the phenomenon of lexical interference, particularly focusing on the incorporation of borrowed determiners from neighboring languages into Francoproven√ßal dialects. Francoproven√ßal, a non-standardized and highly endangered Gallo-Romance language spoken primarily in Switzerland and the Aosta Valley (Italy), presents an intriguing case for linguistic cross-pollination due to its geographic and social proximity to French and Italian. Presenting several maps based on fieldwork data collected across Valais (Switzerland) and the Aosta Valley (Italy), we delineate how these borrowed elements are spatially distributed within different varieties of Francoproven√ßal. Our findings suggest a more intricate pattern than traditionally reported, revealing that some areas exhibit a significant degree of lexical borrowing whereas others remain relatively unaffected. By identifying syntactic contexts where these borrowed determiners appear most frequently, this research provides new insights into the dynamics of language contact, especially in regions dominated by diminishing language communities.

Abstract:
The syntactic characteristics and interdependencies within the verbal complexes of Middle Welsh have often been overshadowed by studies focusing solely on morphological or phonological aspects. This paper seeks to elucidate the intersection between syntax and wordhood in Middle Welsh, analyzing how syntactical contexts influence the recognition and behavior of what could be considered ‚Äòwords‚Äô within this language. By situating our analysis within contemporary typological frameworks on word boundaries and leveraging a corpus-based approach, we provide a new perspective on how sentence-level constructions challenge or reinforce traditional notions of wordhood in Middle Welsh.

### 1. Introduction
In recent years, there has been increasing interest in the linguistic outcomes of heritage speakers, and how their bilingual or multilingual environments influence language retention and shift. Among the diverse diaspora communities across Europe, second-generation speakers of Bosnian/Croatian/Montenegrin/Serbian (BCMS) from the former Yugoslavia present a compelling case for examining these phenomena in-depth. While existing research has focused predominantly on structural properties of their speech and social factors influencing language maintenance, an under-explored yet significant area involves probing into intergenerational lexical convergence within family units. This study seeks to fill this gap by exploring whether and to what extent second-generation BCMS speakers in German-speaking Switzerland align lexically with their first-generation immigrant parents.
The phenomenon of lexical convergence denotes a process where interlocutors adopt similar vocabulary items during communication through exposure or accommodation strategies (Giles & Powesland, 1975). In bilingual settings, such convergence often challenges monolingual norms due to the intricate interplay between two or more active lexicons (Francois Grosjean, 2008). For heritage languages like BCMS spoken amidst a dominant German-speaking milieu in Switzerland, understanding these dynamics is crucial for both theoretical advancements in contact linguistics and practical applications, such as curriculum design for community-based language instruction.
To address this research question compellingly necessitates empirical investigation founded on robust datasets that reveal naturally occurring speech patterns among family members. Specifically, we introduce our "Family Lexicon Convergence Corpus" (FLCC), comprising recorded conversations between six families originally hailing from former Yugoslavia but now residing in Switzerland's German-speaking regions. These recordings capture interactions involving both first-generation immigrants who lived substantial portions of their lives within Yugo-Balkanic cultural spheres before migrating during adulthood (>20 years old) alongside their Swiss-born children raised predominantly under local sociolinguistic influences.

### Introduction
In the realm of cognitive psychology, working memory (WM) is acknowledged as a critical component underlying a range of cognitive functions such as reasoning, learning, and comprehension. Traditionally, WM has been investigated through tasks that require immediate serial recall of words or digits. A substantial body of research finds performance in these tasks to be negatively affected by phonological similarity; items that sound alike (e.g., cat, hat, bat) tend to confuse participants more than those with distinct phonological properties. This phenomenon is referred to as the phonological-similarity effect and is well-documented in the literature (Baddeley et al., 1975). Conversely, semantic similarity‚Äîwhere items share similar meanings but differ phonetically‚Äîdoes not appear to degrade performance equivalently (Shulman et al., 1972).
Despite this bifurcation in our understanding of how WM encodes phonological and semantic information differently, less attention has been given to cross-modal interactions within WM systems that incorporate non-verbal stimuli like visual or auditory signals alongside verbal inputs. Previous works have largely compartmentalized investigations into either purely verbal WM components or purely visual-spatial ones without scrutinizing how mixed-modality contexts influence task performance.
To address this lacuna in our knowledge base, the present study focuses on examining how mixed modality input impacts item-context binding within working memory paradigms. In specific terms:
**Research Question:** How do phonologically and semantically similar words paired with congruent versus incongruent non-verbal stimuli affect retrieval accuracy in item-context binding tasks?
This question stems from theoretical considerations regarding integrative capacity and limitations of WM systems described by Baddeley's multi-component model (2000). According to this model, separate slave systems handle different types of material‚Äîin particular: 
1. The *phonological loop*, which manages verbal information.
2. The *visuo-spatial sketchpad*, which maintains visual-analogical data.
3. The *episodic buffer*, posited as an interface for integrating different sorts of material.
Within the context of item-context bindings‚Äîwhich necessitate engagement across multiple types‚Äîthe episodic buffer's role becomes critically pertinent for investigating cross-modal impacts on retention and recall processes.
Moreover,the Sundberg-L√∂ffler hypothesis elucidates another layer; it postulates that mixed modalities may modulate the associative strength between items based on their contextual integration complexity rather than simple retention effort across isolated channels.
To empirically test these hypothesized models amidst mixed modalities' settings:
1. Phonologically similar word pairs are presented alongside visually congruent images vs incongruent ones.
2. Semantically similar word pairs are subjected under identical conditions for comparison.
